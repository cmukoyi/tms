Accounting Help Guide

What is Double-Entry Bookkeeping?

The accounting module uses double-entry bookkeeping, a system that has been used for over 500 years to ensure accurate financial records.

Key Principle

Every financial transaction affects at least TWO accounts. This creates a complete picture of where money comes from and where it goes.

DEBIT Side

Increases:

  • Assets (Cash, Bank, Inventory, Equipment)
  • Expenses (Salaries, Rent, Utilities)

Decreases:

  • Liabilities (Loans, Accounts Payable)
  • Equity (Owner's Capital)
  • Revenue (Sales, Service Income)
CREDIT Side

Increases:

  • Liabilities (Loans, Accounts Payable)
  • Equity (Owner's Capital)
  • Revenue (Sales, Service Income)

Decreases:

  • Assets (Cash, Bank, Inventory, Equipment)
  • Expenses (Salaries, Rent, Utilities)

Why Must Journal Entries Balance?

The Golden Rule

DEBITS MUST ALWAYS EQUAL CREDITS

This ensures your books are mathematically correct and nothing is missing or entered incorrectly.

Practical Examples:
Example 1: Receiving Money from a Customer (R 1,000)
Account Debit Credit Explanation
Bank Account (Asset) R 1,000.00 - Money coming IN (increase asset)
Sales Revenue (Income) - R 1,000.00 Earned income (increase revenue)
TOTALS R 1,000.00 R 1,000.00 ✓ BALANCED
Example 2: Paying Rent (R 5,000)
Account Debit Credit Explanation
Rent Expense (Expense) R 5,000.00 - Expense incurred (increase expense)
Bank Account (Asset) - R 5,000.00 Money going OUT (decrease asset)
TOTALS R 5,000.00 R 5,000.00 ✓ BALANCED
Example 3: Taking a Loan (R 50,000)
Account Debit Credit Explanation
Bank Account (Asset) R 50,000.00 - Money received (increase asset)
Loan Payable (Liability) - R 50,000.00 Amount owed (increase liability)
TOTALS R 50,000.00 R 50,000.00 ✓ BALANCED

How to Use Each Feature

Purpose: Get a quick overview of your financial position

What you'll see:

  • Total Assets: Everything your company owns (cash, equipment, inventory)
  • Total Liabilities: Everything your company owes (loans, payables)
  • Total Equity: Net worth (Assets - Liabilities)
  • Recent Journal Entries: Latest transactions

Purpose: Manage all your accounting categories

What it is: A complete list of all accounts used to categorize your financial transactions

How to use:

  1. Click "Create New Account"
  2. Select the Account Type (Cash, Bank, Sales, Rent, etc.)
  3. Enter an Account Number (e.g., 1001 for Bank)
  4. Give it a clear Name (e.g., "First National Bank - Checking")
  5. Accounts are automatically categorized by type

Account Numbering Guide:

  • 1000-1999: Assets (Cash, Bank, Inventory)
  • 2000-2999: Liabilities (Loans, Payables)
  • 3000-3999: Equity (Capital, Retained Earnings)
  • 4000-4999: Revenue (Sales, Service Income)
  • 5000-9999: Expenses (Rent, Salaries, Utilities)

Purpose: Record all financial transactions

Step-by-Step Guide:

  1. Click "Create New Entry"
  2. Fill in Details:
    • Date of transaction
    • Description (what happened)
    • Reference number (invoice #, receipt #)
  3. Add Transaction Lines:
    • At least 2 lines required
    • Select the account affected
    • Enter amount in DEBIT or CREDIT (never both)
    • Add line description if needed
  4. Check Totals: Debits MUST equal Credits
  5. Submit
Common Mistake:
Entering the same amount in BOTH debit and credit columns. Choose only ONE side per line.

Quick Tips:

  • Think: "Where is the money coming FROM?" (Credit) and "Where is it GOING TO?" (Debit)
  • For expenses: Debit the expense account, Credit your bank
  • For sales: Debit your bank, Credit sales revenue
  • Use "Add Line" button for transactions affecting more than 2 accounts

Purpose: Shows if your business is making a profit or loss

Formula: Profit = Revenue - Expenses

What it shows:

  • Revenue: All income earned (sales, services)
  • Expenses: All costs incurred (rent, salaries, utilities)
  • Net Income: Bottom line - profit or (loss)

How to use:

  1. Select date range (month, quarter, year)
  2. Review revenue sources
  3. Analyze expense categories
  4. Check if profitable (green) or loss-making (red)

Purpose: Snapshot of your company's financial position at a specific date

Formula: Assets = Liabilities + Equity

What it shows:

  • Assets: What you OWN (cash, equipment, receivables)
  • Liabilities: What you OWE (loans, payables)
  • Equity: What you're WORTH (capital + retained profits)

How to read it:

  • Higher assets vs liabilities = stronger position
  • Positive equity = company has net worth
  • The report always balances mathematically
